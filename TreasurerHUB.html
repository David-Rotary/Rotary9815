The Treasurer's playbook

Eight steps to a financially strong club

Work through each step and tick the actions as you complete them. Together they build the financial footing that lets your club launch projects from strength, not from scratch.

Separating funds is the foundation of good club finance — it's a Rotary requirement and it protects your donors. Operating money (dues, meals) and charitable money (donations, fundraising) must never mix.

Why it matters: money raised for a particular cause must actually be spent on that cause — that's both Rotary policy and the law in Victoria.

Set the year's budget before it starts, ideally led by the President-Elect with the board. The administration account should aim to break even; the charitable account is where your project working capital lives.

A written reserves policy is what gives the club both the confidence and the cash to start projects. The ACNC's position: a reasonable reserve is good practice, there's no universal level, and your board decides what suits the club's risks.

Key point: funds quarantined for a specific donor or appeal are restricted and don't count as free reserves — only unrestricted money can seed your next project. You can hold a surplus, but not hoard it indefinitely.

Most clubs are incorporated associations under the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic), regulated by Consumer Affairs Victoria. If your club is registered with the ACNC, you generally report to the ACNC instead of duplicating to CAV.

Reporting tiers now align with ACNC charity sizes — small (under $500k), medium ($500k–under $3m) and large ($3m+) — for the financial year ending on or after 1 July 2024. Larger clubs face review or audit requirements.

Public fundraising is governed by the Fundraising Act 1998 (Vic) and administered by Consumer Affairs Victoria. You must register as a fundraiser unless you qualify for an exemption.

Common exemption: raising under $20,000 gross a year, using only unpaid volunteers, with nobody paid to fundraise. ACNC-registered charities simply notify CAV of their intention to fundraise rather than registering separately.

Income from hiring out equipment (like art screens) or charging for a service is commercial trading income, not a fundraising appeal — the hirer pays a fair rate and receives value in return. As an incorporated association you can trade where it relates to your purpose; you simply can't pay profits to members.

Two things to get right: because it's commercial, the $20,000 fundraising-exemption threshold doesn't apply to it — but keep it a clean hire (invoice for a service, not a "donation to Rotary"), or it can be drawn into the Fundraising Act. And watch the GST line: an NFP must register for GST once its commercial turnover reaches $150,000 in a rolling 12 months (gifts and donations don't count).

A club account usually can't give donors a tax deduction. Route eligible gifts through Rotary's DGR funds, run by Rotary Australia World Community Service (RAWCS) — that unlocks larger gifts, corporate and ancillary-fund money, plus project accounting and compliance.

For local projects: the Rotary Australia Benevolent Society (RABS) holds DGR status for Australian community projects; the Overseas Aid Fund covers international work. Matched compassionate grants are also available.

Sound controls protect the club's money and its people — and they make the annual handover to the next treasurer simple rather than stressful.

The Rotary year

A treasurer's year at a glance

A simple rhythm for the July–June Rotary year, so nothing important gets left to the last minute.

Jul – Sep

Start strong

  • Take handover and confirm bank access & signatories
  • Adopt the board-approved budget
  • Put the reserves policy in place
Oct – Dec

Steady rhythm

  • Monthly reconciliations & board reports
  • Mid-year financial review
  • Check fundraising registration / notices
Jan – Mar

Fund the projects

  • Track project funding vs plan
  • Plan major fundraisers
  • Confirm RAWCS / DGR registration for projects
Apr – Jun

Close & hand over

  • Prepare year-end statements
  • Arrange review / audit and AGM financials
  • Document a clean handover
