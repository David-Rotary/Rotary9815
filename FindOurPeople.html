Rotary District 9815 · Membership

Every member is a connector.Help us find our people.

Growing the club isn’t the membership director’s job alone — it’s yours too. In five short steps, here’s how to understand why clubs shrink, talk about what we do, and bring the right people alongside us.

Membership directors: share this page with every member. It turns four District discussion papers into one five-minute, do-it-yourself guide — ending with a real action each member can take this month.
Step 1 · The honest picture

It’s not a goodwill problem

Rotary’s decline is real — but the usual explanation (“people don’t care any more’) is wrong. People still care. What’s changed is how, and whether they ever get asked.

~36%
Fall in Australian Rotary membership over 20 years — worse per head of population.
~half
Of all volunteers joined because someone they knew personally asked them.
~60%
Of people don’t even realise a Rotary club exists in their own community.

So the gap isn’t affection — people like what Rotary does. The gap is relationships (we rarely ask) and clarity (people can’t picture themselves in it). A recruitment drive fixes neither.

People volunteer to meet a need — tap one to see it

The same task means different things to different people. Knowing what someone’s really after is how you find — and keep — them.

Takeaway: We’re not short of generous people. We’re short of relationships and clear invitations — and that’s fixable.
Step 2 · A better way to grow

Stop recruiting. Start connecting.

Almost no one joins cold. Build relationships first, and let membership grow out of them. Two shifts make the difference:

Flip 1 — Relationships before asks
Be useful and known in the community before you ask anyone for anything. A club people already work alongside never has to run a “drive.”
Flip 2 — From “what does Rotary do?” to “what could I do?”
Offer people a way to make a difference, not a description of an institution. Shift the question in their mind to “how do I get involved?”

The engagement ladder — tap a rung

Never ask a stranger to join. Ask a participant to do a little more. Match the invitation to the rung.

Visible community projects and presence; tell the story of the difference you make.The ask: none yet — just become trusted.

A single hands-on day a non-member can join with zero strings. They feel the difference first-hand.The ask: “Come help us do this one thing.”
A Friends of the Club / volunteer circle. They’re part of the orbit, turning up regularly.The ask: “Join the team for this cause.”
They’ve found a role that fits. The talk is now about what they bring, not what Rotary is.The ask: “You’re already one of us — make it official?”

Takeaway: Membership is what grows in the soil of relationships. Build the soil first.
Step 3 · Say it so a stranger gets it

Lead with the difference, not the institution

A stranger doesn’t want a definition of Rotary. They want to know what difference you make — and whether they could be part of it.

What to say first … and last

LeadA concrete local difference — a real result, with a face or a place.
LeadThe belief behind it — why you refuse to accept the problem.
SecondHow they could be part of it.
LaterCauses you work on; meeting times; what it costs.
Avoid earlyHistory, global size, structure, jargon (“District”, “the Foundation”…).

Say this, not that

Draws them in

“Come build something that matters, with people you’ll be glad to know.”

Step 4 · From stranger to family

Belonging before obligation

A third of those who leave go within their first two years — usually because they never felt they belonged before the duties arrived. The first 90 days decide it.

The welcome, phase by phase

Before they join · sponsor
Ask what they want from it. Set honest expectations. Introduce them to a few members by name.
First week · buddy
Make contact within days, sit with them, give “no dumb questions” permission.
First 30 days · buddy
One small, meaningful task. One social invite. An honest check-in: “is this what you hoped?”
90 days · highest-risk gate
Match them to a role that fits their motive. Second check-in.
First year · club
Mark the milestone. Ask them to help welcome the next newcomer.

Is the transition working? Watch the signs

  • SociallySeeks people out, learning names
  • Taking partVolunteers, asks how to help
  • VoiceOffers opinions, asks questions
  • LanguageSays “we” and “our club”
  • EnergyEnjoys it, brings a guest
  • Effort vs rewardFeels their contribution matters
Takeaway: The shift from “we” to “you” is the clearest warning sign. Act early — drift rarely reverses itself.
Step 5 · Your part this month

One member, one invitation

The single highest-yield thing any of us can do costs nothing: think of one person who’d care, and invite them to do something — not to join.
